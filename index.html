Digital Culture
The course's objective is to bring students to question a chosen aspect of their digital lives by:

  • Choosing a specific phenomenon which at least partly relies on having an online presence
  • Exploring its features to bring out yet undiscovered/undisclosed data
  • Exploiting this data to shed a new light on the chosen item
1 - Explorations
Students are expected to change their perspective on a particular feature of their online identity. Shifting from a user to researcher status is the first step on the path to critical exploration. That means crafting hypotheses and designing a method to validate or invalidate their underlyings.
Lead examples
What should students be looking for? Some examples, batched by theme, are listed below:
  • #Territories
  • #PornVsReality
  • #NationBuilder
  • #MeToo

#Territories
  • Flux/flows going through the train station/harbour? Humans or goods? Quantifiable or not? Manageable or not? By whom?
  • Educating about environmental/public safety issues
  • How do people move, among BlablaCar, Uber, bicycle apps, or more traditional means
  • Online/local territories: graph/hip-hop crews
  • Mapping the local UNESCO heritage on the ground vs online
  • Similar Selfies: finding photo spots liked by tourists and aggregate identical pictures using GPS coordinates and/or ML
#PornVsReality
  • Online generalist platforms with “massage” sections connecting freelance sex workers with potential clients (for a fee)
  • “Amateur” porn shot by local/national studios and branded as such.
  • The past, present and future of streaming: from CamGirls&Boys to twitch videogame players? Unknown global streamers or local celebrities?
  • Deepfake democratization: from movie starts to coworkers/classmates, what are the social impacts?
  • Tinder vs Meetic: connecting humans with romantic intents
#NationBuilder
  • Politics/politicians in the age of (ageing?) social media/echo chambers/blogs.
  • Grassroots movements, between radical online/local communities and wider groups (i.e. national political parties).
  • Politics & Videogames: shaping a political indentity through border control, migration, war as a civilian or brexit ?
  • Viral messages and truth, a complicated relationship
  • Delivering the internet by USB to NK
  • Gypsies up for a fight, from YouTube to violence on the ground
#MeToo
  • From a global movement to a local reality?
  • Feminism in connected smaller cities/communities
#Cash&Trash
  • Amazon versus La Galerne
  • Hotels versus Airbnb/Booking and the like
  • Comments/Stars/Ratings, building trust among fake reviews and bot farms
  • Delivering goods and services: freelancers unite?
#Meta
  • Twitter use in the near area compared to other areas in the country, or different comparable areas
  • Information flow: where does the news come from in this area?
  • Information security: what is available/accessible on the internet? Are my passwords public? Has my identity been compromised to some extent?
  • Local YouTubers/Instagramers?
  • Local blogers?
2 - Finding/refining data
What information can you get on your target? What can be quantitatively or qualitatively measured? What variables can you isolate? Can you interview its users? Access some the services' data? Be inventive, try not to use proxies.
3 - Shedding a new light
Find the most relevant way to present your results. Grab your public's attention and guide them through an adventure that will make them understand why the phenomenon you studied is also crucial to their own (digital) lives.
Squad system
Students are grouped by squads. Squads are premade by teachers. 75% of the final grade is common to a squad. Freeriding is dangerous: the squad as a whole is designed to have major skill gaps to overcome as a team. If crew members are missing, the boat just won't float. Squads will work using a private channel/thread (accessible by the TA) on a message service.
Grades
The final grade is based on your group thread and the final output. The grade is built as a sequence: if the previous item is mediocre, chances that the following ones are good are probably quite thin. Any weakness in the final output will be linked to one of those items, and will therefore be documented by your discussion in your group thread.
Rules
Students are obviously expected to abide by the same framework of rules in use for any other course.
Final remarks
  • Students are given access to 6 online lectures covering the Digital Culture syllabus.
  • Students will attend two days of technology workshops, covering topics such as data management, HTML/CSS/JS basics, video editing and the like.
  • Each team will meet with a referent TA at least twice during the semester.
  • Each team will be granted access to an online chat server (Slack/Discord), where they can organise and ask for a TA's help. Schedule and important information will be dispatched on a common channel.
  • Each team is expected to produce a digital exploration to be published on the university's internal network. The most interesting ones might be shared to a wider audience.
